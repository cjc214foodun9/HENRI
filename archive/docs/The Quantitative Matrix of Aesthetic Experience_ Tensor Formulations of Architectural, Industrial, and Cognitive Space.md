**Chapter 5: Chromatic Opponent Modeling and Spectral Image Analysis**  
Color relationships in classical and modern art operate on physical wave properties and human perceptual limits.\[45, 46\] Analyzing these systems requires evaluating both chromatic opposition and the spatial distribution of color frequencies.\[46, 47\]  
**Chromatic Opposition and Opponent Color Networks**  
The human visual system processes color through three opponent channels: red-green, blue-yellow, and black-white.\[46\] These opponent pairs cannot be perceived simultaneously in the same spatial point; for example, a color is never described as "reddish-green".\[46\]  
This opponent architecture was explored by Johann Wolfgang von Goethe and later formalized by Johannes Itten in his Chromatic Sphere.\[45, 47\] Itten's system organizes reference colors across five levels of intensity and three levels of saturation to analyze primary contrasts \[47\]:

* **Complementary Contrast**: Pairs hues positioned directly opposite one another on the color wheel (e.g., red and green, or yellow and violet), maximizing chromatic contrast and visual vibration.\[45, 48\]  
* **Temperature Contrast**: Groups colors into "warm" (red-orange-yellow) and "cool" (blue-green-violet) zones, which artists use to create depth and volume without relying on traditional light-and-shadow (*chiaroscuro*) techniques.\[47, 48\]

A key development in classical painting was the transition from single-pigment carbon blacks to "chromatic black".\[49\] Created by mixing complementary colors (such as ultramarine blue and burnt umber), chromatic black absorbs most wavelengths of light but still reflects subtle undertones.\[49\] This prevents the flat, light-absorbing appearance of carbon-based pigments, giving shadows a sense of depth, temperature, and atmospheric vibration.\[49\]
