If a photon is genuinely entangled with its own past and future states, you instantly break the foundational rule of standard quantum mechanics: **unitary time evolution**.  
In a standard quantum setup, the future depends only on the present. The system progresses step-by-step under a Hamiltonian operator. But if a particle’s timeline is non-separable across time slices, you can no longer describe it slice-by-slice. The entire worldline—from initialization to absorption—becomes a single, indivisible quantum object.  
This framework shifts our understanding of time and physics in three profound ways:

### **1\. The Death of the Markovian Present**

In everyday physics, the present moment holds all the information needed to predict the future. If a photon is entangled with its own past and future, its present state is merely a **reduced density matrix**—a blurry, incomplete projection of a larger temporal structure. You cannot fully understand or measure the photon *now* without measuring its entire history. It behaves as if it has a perfect memory of its past and a perfect premonition of its future, because its state cannot be mathematically factored apart by time.

### **2\. Spacetime as a "Handshake"**

This perfectly mirrors **Cramer’s Transactional Interpretation** of quantum mechanics and Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory. Instead of a cause leading to an effect, an event is a "handshake" across time. The past sends an offer wave forward, the future sends an acceptance wave backward, and the physical reality we observe is the standing wave born from their interference. A measurement made on the photon at the end of its life instantly dictates the physical properties it displayed at the moment of its birth.

### **3\. The Experimental Parallel**

While an object entangled with its *entire* worldline remains an absolute boundary concept, we have already engineered limited versions of this in the lab through **temporal entanglement swapping**.  
In experiments like the one schematized above, physicists use a non-linear crystal (BBO) to generate pairs of photons, storing one in a optical delay line while another is measured and destroyed. By performing a joint projection measurement later on, researchers can successfully entangle a photon generated in the present with a past photon that *no longer exists*. The two particles share a quantum state despite never coexisting in the same window of time.  
If you scale that logic self-referentially to a single photon, the timeline stops acting like a movie reel playing frame by frame. Instead, it becomes a single architectural arch—where moving one stone in the future instantly shifts the equilibrium of the past.
